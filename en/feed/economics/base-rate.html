Everything About the Important Macroeconomic Indicator, the Base Rate ‚Äã

Economics Feed | 2025.01.10

The Base Rate generally refers to the interest rate applied between a country's central bank and commercial banks. The base rate is a crucial economic indicator that significantly impacts a country's financial and economic landscape. This article thoroughly explores the definitions of the base rate in South Korea and the U.S., as well as the economic effects and roles of base rate fluctuations.

üí° 3-Point Summary

  • The Base Rate generally refers to the interest rate applied between a country's central bank and commercial banks.
  • The Bank of Korea's Monetary Policy Board determines the base rate eight times a year by comprehensively considering price trends, domestic and global economic conditions, and financial market circumstances.
  • The base rate significantly impacts not only commercial banks' loan and deposit rates but also most macroeconomic conditions such as inflation and exchange rates.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Definition of the Base Rate ‚Äã

The Base Rate generally refers to the interest rate applied between a country's central bank and commercial banks. Specifically, it is the rate applied when commercial banks like Shinhan Bank borrow money from the Bank of Korea. The base rate serves as a reference point for determining loan and deposit rates, impacting all interest rates in daily life.

üá∞üá∑ Definition of South Korea's Base Rate ‚Äã

The Bank of Korea's base rate is a policy rate that serves as the standard for transactions such as repurchase agreements (RP) and liquidity adjustment deposits and loans with financial institutions.

Bank of Korea's Base Rate

  • The Bank of Korea uses the base rate as the fixed bid rate for 7-day RP sales and the minimum bid rate for 7-day RP purchases.
  • The liquidity adjustment deposit rate is the base rate minus 50 basis points (with a minimum rate of 0%), and the liquidity adjustment loan rate is the base rate plus 50 basis points (or double the base rate if below 1%).

The Monetary Policy Board of the Bank of Korea determines the base rate eight times a year after thoroughly reviewing price trends, domestic and global economic conditions, and financial market factors. This board consists of seven members, including the Governor and Deputy Governor of the Bank of Korea.

Once set, the base rate immediately affects ultra-short-term rates like the call rate, eventually influencing short- and long-term market rates, as well as deposit and loan rates, thereby impacting real economic activities. Due to its direct impact on the economy, the base rate is typically adjusted incrementally by 0.25%p.

Emergency Measures

  • However, in cases of severe external shocks like COVID-19, the base rate may be adjusted drastically.
  • After COVID-19, the U.S. Federal Reserve and the Bank of Korea made large-scale adjustments of 0.75%p and 0.50%p, respectively.

üá∫üá∏ Definition of the U.S. Base Rate ‚Äã

The U.S. base rate refers to the Federal Funds Rate determined by the Federal Reserve (Fed). This is the interest rate at which commercial banks in the U.S. lend and borrow excess reserves overnight.

The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) adjusts the federal funds rate to achieve price stability and maximize employment. The U.S. base rate has a significant influence on global financial markets and affects the flow of the U.S. dollar and global capital markets.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Role of the Base Rate ‚Äã

The base rate plays various roles across the economy.

The primary roles of the base rate are:

  1. Monetary Policy Tool:
    The base rate is a key instrument for adjusting money supply and market interest rates.
    An increase in the base rate absorbs money in the market, cooling an overheated economy.
    A decrease in the base rate injects liquidity, revitalizing the economy.
  2. Price Stability:
    Adjusting the base rate helps maintain inflation at an appropriate level.
  3. Economic Growth Adjustment:
    Interest rate changes help prevent economic overheating or recession.
  4. Financial Market Stability:
    It stabilizes financial markets by controlling liquidity.
  5. Exchange Rate Adjustment:
    Interest rate changes affect exchange rates, balancing imports and exports.

The base rate is a crucial policy tool that determines the direction and flow of the economy. Central banks use it to pursue stable economic growth and maintain financial market stability.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Impact of a Rising Base Rate ‚Äã

When the base rate rises, market interest rates and loan rates also increase. This raises funding costs for businesses and households, potentially contracting consumption and investment.

Specific impacts include:

  1. Reduced Consumption: Higher loan interest burdens reduce household spending.
  2. Decreased Corporate Investment: Increased funding costs curb corporate investment.
  3. Sluggish Real Estate Market: Rising mortgage rates dampen the housing market.
  4. Stronger Currency: Higher rates can lead to currency appreciation.

Rate hikes are mainly used to prevent economic overheating and stabilize inflation.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Impact of a Falling Base Rate ‚Äã

When the base rate falls, market interest rates and loan rates drop. This encourages consumption and investment, boosting the economy.

Specific impacts include:

  1. Increased Consumption: Lower loan interest boosts household spending.
  2. Expanded Corporate Investment: Reduced funding costs promote business expansion.
  3. Revitalized Real Estate Market: Lower mortgage rates drive housing demand.
  4. Weaker Currency: Rate cuts can lead to currency depreciation.

A base rate cut is a vital policy tool to prevent economic downturns and stimulate growth.


üöß Real-world Economic Effects May Vary

  • The described effects are general economic responses to base rate movements.
  • Actual impacts can differ depending on the economic environment at the time.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Can the Base Rate Become Negative? ‚Äã

After the 2008 financial crisis, some countries began adopting a negative base rate. Sweden was the first to introduce negative interest rates in 2009, followed by the European Central Bank in 2014 and Japan in 2016.

Setting the base rate to negative means that the central bank lends money to commercial banks while effectively paying them to borrow, whereas banks must pay fees if they leave their funds with the central bank.

In other words, a negative interest rate signals that the central bank is willing to lend aggressively to commercial banks to inject liquidity into the market and boost consumption to revive the economy. While it demonstrates the central bank‚Äôs strong commitment to economic recovery, it also reflects an exceptional and extreme situation.

6Ô∏è‚É£ Conclusion ‚Äã

üè¶ Impact on the Market

  • When the base rate increases, it absorbs liquidity from the market, reducing the money supply and cooling down an overheated economy. Conversely, when the base rate decreases, it injects liquidity into the market, expanding the money supply and revitalizing the economy. An interest rate hike can also cause stock prices to fall and the exchange rate to drop, while a rate cut can lead to the opposite effect.
  • However, these are theoretical effects, and actual economic outcomes may differ significantly. Policymakers often choose between monetary policy, represented by interest rates, and fiscal policy, represented by government spending. This choice typically depends on the political orientation of the ruling administration and directly influences the national economy.

üí∏ Impact on Market Interest Rates

  • When the base rate rises, various market interest rates also rise. Conversely, when the base rate falls, market interest rates also decline, and this effect is often deliberately induced. However, since the base rate is merely a target rate, actual market interest rates are determined by the supply and demand of funds.
  • The Bank of Korea influences inflation and money supply directly through monetary policies such as bond trading and adjustments to reserve requirements or the rediscount rate. Additionally, financial institutions, including commercial banks, reference the base rate when setting their interest rates.
  • Therefore, it is common for market interest rates to rise when the base rate rises and for market rates to fall when the base rate falls. Although the Bank of Korea‚Äôs base rate directly impacts domestic bank interest rates, changes in the U.S. Federal Reserve‚Äôs base rate significantly influence the Bank of Korea‚Äôs decisions. This is due to the need to manage large-scale capital inflows or outflows in response to changes in the U.S. base rate.
