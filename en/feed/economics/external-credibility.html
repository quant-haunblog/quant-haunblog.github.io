What is External Credibility? ​

Economics Feed | 2024.12.16

External credibility is a measure of a country's economic trustworthiness. It is reflected in the National Credit Rating assigned by international credit rating agencies, representing the global evaluation of a country's debt repayment ability and economic stability. This serves as a key factor in determining how reliably and affordably a nation can secure funds in the global financial market.

🅰️ National Credit Rating

  • International credit rating agencies such as Standard & Poor’s (S&P), Moody’s, and Fitch assess a country’s debt repayment ability and economic stability to assign a credit rating. Major ratings range from AAA (highest) to D (default) and are classified into investment grades (AAA to BBB) and speculative grades (BB and below). Credit ratings directly impact the cost of issuing government bonds, foreign currency inflows, and international credibility.

1️⃣ How is External Credibility Assessed? ​

The assessment of external credibility is based on a comprehensive analysis of economic, political, and diplomatic factors.

1️⃣ Economic Factors

  • Economic Growth Rate: A stable and consistent growth rate reflects the vitality of a country’s economy. Higher and steadier growth rates are viewed positively in credibility evaluations.
  • Fiscal Soundness: The government’s debt ratio and management capabilities are key evaluation criteria. Excessive debt increases credit risk, while an appropriate debt level and efficient management enhance trustworthiness.
  • Foreign Exchange Reserves: These indicate a country's ability to stabilize the foreign exchange market and respond to external shocks. Ample reserves reduce the likelihood of currency crises and contribute to higher credibility.
  • Trade Balance: The trade balance represents the economic gains a country generates through international trade. A sustained trade surplus indicates consistent foreign currency inflows and stable economic growth, positively influencing credit ratings.

2️⃣ Political Factors

  • Political Stability: A stable environment where the government can implement policies consistently is essential for maintaining credibility. Political turmoil or abrupt policy changes due to regime shifts negatively impact credit ratings.
  • Policy Transparency and Reliability: Predictable and transparent governance builds trust among international investors. Conversely, corruption and opaque policy operations undermine credibility.
  • Level of Social Conflict: Large-scale protests or political unrest are considered risk factors in credit evaluations. A stable social environment contributes to enhancing a country's trustworthiness.

3️⃣ Diplomatic Factors

  • Alliances and International Cooperation: Strong alliances enhance a nation's external stability and support credit rating improvements. For instance, South Korea has gained international trust through its alliance with the United States.
  • Adherence to Trade Agreements and Global Norms: Compliance with international trade rules and active cooperation with other nations also boost external credibility. South Korea has earned high marks by faithfully implementing major trade agreements, such as the Korea-U.S. FTA.

2️⃣ Why is External Credibility Important? ​

External credibility significantly impacts exchange rate stability and the inflow of foreign investments. Countries with high credit ratings are perceived as stable investment destinations, encouraging active foreign investments. As foreign investment increases, a steady inflow of foreign currency helps maintain stable exchange rates. For example, after the 1997 Asian financial crisis, South Korea substantially increased its foreign exchange reserves, maintaining high credibility and successfully reducing exchange rate volatility.

Nations with high credit ratings can borrow money at lower interest rates, greatly reducing funding costs. This lessens fiscal burdens and allows governments to allocate funds efficiently to critical areas such as infrastructure, welfare, and technological development—driving economic growth. On the other hand, countries with lower credit ratings are considered high-risk for investments, forcing them to borrow at higher interest rates. This leads to increased financial burdens and a higher likelihood of economic crises. South Korea, maintaining an AA rating, has been able to secure low-interest financing and invest in key sectors like education, welfare, and infrastructure.

High external credibility also serves as a significant advantage in trade and diplomatic negotiations. South Korea has maintained a stable and high credit rating from international credit agencies, establishing itself as a trustworthy partner in major trade agreements and investment initiatives. This credibility has bolstered its negotiation power in agreements such as the Korea-U.S. FTA, enabling the country to secure favorable terms based on its reputation as a reliable partner.

3️⃣ The Evolution of South Korea’s External Credibility ​

🆘 The Asian Financial Crisis and the Credibility Crisis ​

The 1997 Asian financial crisis highlighted the critical importance of external credibility for the South Korean economy. At the time, South Korea faced a significant downgrade in its national credit rating due to factors such as excessive short-term debt, insufficient foreign exchange reserves, and a lack of transparency in the financial system. Leading credit rating agencies like S&P and Moody's downgraded South Korea’s rating to speculative grade, triggering a massive outflow of foreign capital and economic turmoil.

Following the IMF bailout, South Korea succeeded in restoring its credibility by improving its economic structure and implementing reforms. The government significantly increased foreign exchange reserves, strengthened fiscal soundness, and enhanced financial system transparency. As a result, South Korea regained its pre-crisis credit rating levels, achieving an AA rating from S&P in 2012, marking advanced-nation-level trust.

🛡️ The Global Financial Crisis and Defending Credibility ​

During the 2008 global financial crisis, South Korea demonstrated resilience by maintaining its external credibility. With adequate foreign exchange reserves and fiscal capacity, South Korea sustained its stable external credibility despite global financial instability. The government and the Bank of Korea implemented proactive foreign exchange stabilization policies, which minimized exchange rate volatility and preserved foreign investor confidence.

🅰️ The Presidential Impeachment and Credit Stability ​

The 2016 impeachment of former President Park Geun-hye showcased how political crises can have limited impact on credibility when economic stability and institutional trust are strong. Despite the political turmoil, South Korea maintained its credit rating thanks to economic stability and institutional reliability. This resilience is one of the key reasons why the South Korean economy is internationally trusted.

4️⃣ December 3rd: The Martial Law Declaration and External Credibility ​

On December 3, 2024, President Yoon Suk-yeol's declaration of a state of emergency stirred significant domestic and international concern. Although the martial law was lifted within six hours due to the National Assembly's swift resolution, the incident had a negative impact on South Korea's external credibility.

🏢 The Assessment of International Credit Rating Agencies ​

International credit rating agency Standard & Poor's (S&P) assessed that the state of emergency would have "no significant immediate impact" on the country’s credit rating. S&P stated, "Since the martial law was lifted within hours and South Korea's institutional foundation remains solid, we are maintaining the current 'AA' rating."

However, S&P also noted that the incident does not guarantee that future credit assessments will be unaffected. "This was an entirely unexpected event, and foreign investors may become more cautious about investing in South Korea," they added. Moody’s also warned that "if political tensions persist, it could negatively affect South Korea’s credit rating and domestic assets." However, concerns over prolonged political unrest were somewhat eased following the passage of an impeachment motion against President Yoon on December 14, reducing fears of a prolonged impeachment crisis.

🏃 The Swift Response of Financial Authorities ​

Following the December 3rd martial law declaration, the South Korean government took a series of measures to stabilize the financial markets. The Bank of Korea purchased over 10 trillion won worth of repurchase agreements (RPs) daily to inject liquidity into the market. Simultaneously, the government activated the Bond Market Stabilization Fund (BMSF) and the Securities Market Stabilization Fund (SMSF) to alleviate volatility in the bond and stock markets.

💡 Bond Market Stabilization Fund (BMSF)

  • A fund established to stabilize the bond market during a financial crisis. In times of crisis, the BMSF buys government bonds, public bonds, and corporate bonds to inject liquidity into the market, easing rapid interest rate hikes and credit tightening. This allows companies to secure funds more stably and alleviates the psychological unease of bond investors.

💡 Securities Market Stabilization Fund (SMSF)

  • A fund used to stabilize the stock market when investor sentiment worsens, such as during sharp stock market declines. Through the SMSF, stocks are purchased to prevent further drops in stock prices, with the aim of restoring investor sentiment and strengthening market trust. Major financial institutions contribute funds to operate the SMSF, which is deployed when market volatility becomes severe.

Financial authorities also took active steps to stabilize the foreign exchange market. The government and the Bank of Korea used foreign exchange reserves to supply dollars to the foreign exchange market, preventing a sharp rise in the exchange rate and curbing foreign exchange volatility. These measures played a critical role in stabilizing the foreign exchange market and reducing exchange rate fluctuations. While these actions helped gradually restore stability to the financial market, short-term shocks, such as the outflow of foreign capital and a decline in stock prices, were inevitable.

💡 3-Point Summary

  • External credibility represents a nation's economic trustworthiness and is assessed through credit ratings.
  • External credibility significantly impacts the financial market, including exchange rate stability and reduced government bond issuance costs.
  • The December 3rd martial law declaration is a representative example of how political instability negatively affects external credibility.
