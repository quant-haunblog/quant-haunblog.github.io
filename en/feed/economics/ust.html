Understanding U.S. Treasury Yields ​

Economics Feed | 2024.12.09

U.S. Treasury yields are one of the key indicators of the global economy, and they are an important tool for understanding the stability of financial markets and the economic outlook. The fluctuations in U.S. Treasury yields have a major impact on the global economy and financial markets, particularly on financial interactions with emerging markets like South Korea.

Recent increases in U.S. Treasury yields have triggered capital outflows and financial instability domestically. Therefore, economic agents closely monitor U.S. Treasury yield trends and explore ways to respond to these changes.

💡 Bond

  • A bond is a certificate issued by a government, public institution, or corporation after borrowing money from individuals. The issuer pays interest to the investor at regular intervals and repays the principal at maturity. The principal is guaranteed if the bond is held until maturity. Most bonds are issued with a fixed interest rate, which remains unchanged after issuance. In the bond market, interest rates and bond prices generally move in opposite directions; as interest rates rise, bond prices fall, and as interest rates fall, bond prices rise.

The Role of U.S. Treasury Yields ​

U.S. Treasury yields are a key indicator for understanding global financial markets. As a risk-free rate, they serve as a benchmark for evaluating investment returns and are used to predict long-term economic conditions and market interest rates. The 10-year Treasury yield is considered a representative measure of U.S. Treasury yields.

⚖️ Investment Return Benchmark

  • U.S. Treasury bonds are considered one of the safest investment assets in the world due to their minimal default risk and low credit risk. Therefore, U.S. Treasury yields are often viewed as risk-free rates and serve as the benchmark for evaluating the returns of other investments. U.S. Treasury yields act as a standard for interest rates globally, serving as the reference point for mortgage rates, corporate bond yields, and other long-term borrowing rates.

💡 Risk-Free Rate

  • A risk-free rate refers to the return on an asset with little to no risk of fluctuation, ensuring predictable returns. Government-issued Treasury bonds and bank deposits are typical examples of such assets. In contrast, stocks, which fluctuate in value based on future economic conditions or company performance, are considered risky assets. However, if a country or bank goes into default, Treasury bonds or bank deposits can become risky assets as well. While there is no such thing as a truly risk-free asset, Treasury bonds are considered risk-free because their risk is negligible.

🗓️ Leading Economic Indicator

  • The difference between long-term and short-term Treasury yields can indicate potential economic recessions or recoveries. Typically, long-term bond yields are higher than short-term yields because bonds with longer maturities are subject to greater risks, such as inflation. A reduction or inversion of the yield curve is seen as a signal of economic slowdown or recession. If long-term growth prospects worsen, concerns about corporate performance increase. Investors may sell stocks and buy long-term Treasury bonds, driving up demand for long-term bonds, which causes long-term yields to fall and the yield curve to flatten. Conversely, an expanding yield curve is seen as a sign of economic recovery.

💸 Long-Term Yields Reflect Inflation Expectations

  • Inflation expectations play a crucial role when investing in long-term financial products. If high inflation is anticipated, Treasury yields rise. This is because inflation erodes the value of future money, and Treasury yields reflect these inflation expectations. When inflation is expected to rise, investors may reduce their Treasury bond holdings, fearing lower real returns. As demand for Treasury bonds decreases, yields increase.

🔟 The 10-Year Treasury Yield is a Key Indicator

  • Among U.S. Treasury yields, the 10-year yield is particularly widely used. The 10-year Treasury yield represents the interest rate for mid-term bonds (between short and long-term) and provides important information for economic forecasting and investment decisions. By observing the U.S. Treasury yield curve, which displays yields for bonds with various maturities (e.g., 1-year, 5-year, 10-year, 30-year), one can gauge economic outlooks and the state of financial markets.

Factors Affecting U.S. Treasury Yields ​

U.S. Treasury yields fluctuate based on the supply and demand for Treasury bonds. The supply of Treasury bonds depends on the amount issued by the government, while demand is influenced by various factors, including shifts in confidence in the government, changes in monetary policy, and market uncertainty.

🏛️ Government Bond Issuance

  • When the U.S. government issues more Treasury bonds to fund its expenditures, the supply in the bond market increases. According to the principles of supply and demand, if the demand for Treasury bonds exceeds the supply, bond prices rise and yields fall. Conversely, if demand does not keep up with the supply, bond prices fall and yields rise.

😥 Government Debt Payment Capacity

  • When a government's debt level becomes unsustainable, investor confidence in Treasury bonds can diminish, as the government's ability to meet its obligations comes into question. If credit rating agencies downgrade the government’s credit rating due to rising debt, Treasury yields may face upward pressure. This has happened in the past when the U.S. government’s credit rating was downgraded, leading to an increase in Treasury yields.

🪙 Changes in Monetary Policy

  • The U.S. Federal Reserve's monetary policy has a significant impact on Treasury yields. When the Federal Reserve raises its benchmark interest rate, investors may worry that the value of their existing Treasury bonds will fall. This increases demand for newly issued Treasury bonds, leading to higher yields. In fact, a series of rate hikes by the Fed in recent years has led to a significant rise in Treasury yields.

⚠️ Market Uncertainty

  • During periods of expected economic slowdown, investors tend to move their funds into safe-haven assets like U.S. Treasury bonds. At the same time, central banks may implement accommodative monetary policies, such as lowering short-term interest rates, which boosts demand for Treasury bonds and drives down yields. However, bond prices and yields generally move in opposite directions. As Treasury yields fall, the prices of previously issued bonds with higher yields rise, leading investors to sell their bonds for profit. If the supply of Treasury bonds increases, bond prices fall, and Treasury yields rise again.

The Impact of U.S. Treasury Yield Movements on the Global Economy ​

The fluctuations in U.S. Treasury yields have a profound impact on the global economy. As one of the safest assets in the world, U.S. Treasury bonds influence financial markets globally. For example, borrowing costs increase, capital flows out of emerging markets, and exchange rates fluctuate.

😭 Increased Borrowing Costs

  • Many countries, including emerging markets, hold significant amounts of dollar-denominated debt. A sharp rise in U.S. Treasury yields increases the cost of servicing this debt, which can lead to sustainability issues. When companies and governments must pay higher costs to borrow money, global investment and economic growth slow down.

😧 Capital Outflows from Emerging Markets

  • When U.S. Treasury yields rise, foreign investors are attracted to the higher returns and tend to invest in U.S. Treasury bonds. The relatively safe U.S. Treasury bonds become more attractive compared to riskier assets like stocks or bonds from emerging markets. This can lead to capital outflows from emerging markets, creating financial instability.

💱 Exchange Rate Fluctuations

  • Changes in U.S. Treasury yields can also affect exchange rates. As foreign investment in U.S. Treasury bonds increases due to higher yields, the value of the U.S. dollar rises. A stronger dollar can make U.S. exports more expensive, reducing demand for U.S. goods and services abroad. Additionally, if commodity prices like oil or metals, priced predominantly in U.S. dollars, rise, global demand may decrease.

In this article, we’ve explored the role of U.S. Treasury yields, the factors influencing them, and their impact on the global economy. The U.S. Treasury market is a key indicator of financial stability and trust in global financial markets. A sharp rise or fall in U.S. Treasury yields can lead to global financial instability. Therefore, understanding and tracking U.S. Treasury yields is essential for making informed decisions in investment, economic forecasting, and financial stability assessment.

💡 3-Point Summary

  • U.S. Treasury yields are a key indicator in global financial markets, and U.S. Treasury bonds are considered one of the safest investments.
  • U.S. Treasury yields fluctuate based on supply and demand, with factors like government debt capacity, monetary policy, and market uncertainty affecting them.
  • A sharp rise in U.S. Treasury yields increases borrowing costs, triggers capital outflows from emerging markets, and causes financial instability.
