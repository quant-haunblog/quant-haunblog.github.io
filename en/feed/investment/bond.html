Understanding Bond Investment ​

Investment Feed | 2024.12.13

When thinking of investment, stocks are often the first to come to mind. But what about bonds? Bonds may feel unfamiliar to some, but with proper knowledge, they can be a smart and safe investment option.

Recently, as central banks worldwide begin to lower interest rates, the bond market is turning green. If you’re new to bonds but curious about their investment potential, this guide will walk you through the basics of bond investing, market outlooks, and more.

1️⃣ What is a Bond? ​

A bond is a certificate issued by governments, corporations, or public institutions to raise funds. Essentially, it is a type of loan agreement in which the issuer promises to pay periodic interest and return the principal at maturity.
Bonds offer investors stable interest income and are classified as safe assets due to their relatively low risk. Let’s explore the different types of bonds and how they generate income.

💡 Key Points About Bonds

  • Bond issuers act as borrowers, while bondholders act as lenders.
  • Bonds pay regular interest and return the principal at maturity.
  • Bond prices and interest rates have an inverse relationship.

🗂️ Types of Bonds ​

Bonds can be categorized based on the issuer, interest rate structure, and maturity period.

Classification by Issuer

  • Bonds can be issued by governments, local governments, public institutions, corporations, and financial institutions. Typically, government bonds are considered the safest.
    • Government Bonds: Issued by national governments and considered the safest bonds.
    • Municipal Bonds: Issued by local governments to support public projects.
    • Financial Bonds: Issued by banks and other financial institutions.
    • Corporate Bonds: Issued by corporations to raise funds for business operations.

Classification by Interest Rate

  • Bonds can have either fixed or variable interest rates, known as the coupon rate.
    • Fixed-Rate Bonds: Offer a fixed interest rate until maturity.
    • Variable-Rate Bonds: The interest rate fluctuates based on market conditions.

Classification by Maturity

  • Bonds can be categorized by their maturity period:
    • Short-Term Bonds: Maturity of 1 year or less.
    • Medium-Term Bonds: Maturity of 1–5 years.
    • Long-Term Bonds: Maturity of over 5 years.

💰 Bond Income Structure ​

Bonds generate income in two ways: interest income and capital gains. Interest income is the fixed return received regularly, while capital gains come from selling bonds at a higher price than their purchase value.
The yield of a bond is determined by its price, but if the issuer defaults, both returns and principal may be lost.

Interest Income

  • The coupon rate is the agreed-upon interest rate at the time of issuance. Even if bond prices fluctuate, the coupon rate typically remains unchanged. Holding the bond to maturity ensures receipt of the principal and promised interest.
  • Bonds like coupon bonds pay periodic interest, providing regular income similar to a salary.

Capital Gains

  • Unlike the coupon rate, bond prices are constantly changing. Bonds can be traded before maturity, allowing investors to profit by buying low and selling high. This flexibility sets bonds apart from fixed-term deposits.
  • Selling bonds during periods of falling market interest rates can lead to capital gains as bond prices rise.

Credit Ratings

  • Although bonds are generally considered safe assets, issuers can default (fail to repay). As such, credit ratings are crucial in assessing bond safety. Credit rating agencies assign grades based on the issuer’s creditworthiness.
  • Investment-Grade Bonds: Lower risk but lower yields.
  • High-Yield (Junk) Bonds: Higher risk but potentially higher returns.

2️⃣ The Relationship Between Bonds and Interest Rates ​

To invest successfully in bonds, it is essential to understand the inverse relationship between bond prices and interest rates.

  • The term "interest rate" here refers to the market interest rate, not the coupon rate set when the bond is issued.
  • If you buy a bond with a coupon rate of 5% when market rates are also at 5%, its value remains stable. However, if market rates rise to 7%, the appeal of your bond diminishes since new bonds now offer higher returns.
  • Consequently, if you need to sell the bond, you may have to lower its price, explaining why rising interest rates decrease bond prices.

📉 Interest Rate Changes and Bond Prices

  • When interest rates rise: The appeal of existing bonds diminishes, leading to price declines.
  • When interest rates fall: The value of existing bonds increases due to their relatively higher fixed coupon rates.

3️⃣ How to Invest in Bonds ​

Bonds can be invested in through either direct or indirect methods. Choose the method that aligns with your experience and preferences.

💁‍♂️ 1️⃣ Methods of Bond Investment ​

Most bonds are initially sold to institutional investors, who then distribute them to the market, making them accessible to individual investors.

Direct Investment

  • This involves buying and selling bonds directly through a brokerage account. Direct investors must research the bond market to make informed decisions.
  • Bonds can be traded via mobile trading systems (MTS) or home trading systems (HTS) like stocks.
    • Exchange-Traded Bonds: Available in public markets.
    • Over-the-Counter Bonds: Offered by specific brokers.
  • Direct investments are tax-advantaged: capital gains are exempt from taxes, although interest income is subject to a 15.4% tax.

Indirect Investment

  • This involves investing in financial products like bond funds through brokers or banks, making it suitable for beginners.
  • Bond Funds: Diversified portfolios managed by professionals.
  • Bond ETFs: Exchange-traded funds that allow stock-like trading with lower costs.

🪜 Bond Investment Strategies ​

Once you decide on a method, consider strategies such as the barbell or ladder approach.

Barbell Strategy

  • Invest in both low-risk, short-term bonds and high-risk, high-yield bonds. This diversification helps mitigate risks across market conditions.

Ladder Strategy

  • Divide investments equally across bonds with staggered maturities. For example, allocate equal amounts to 1-, 3-, 5-, and 10-year bonds. This ensures liquidity while managing risks.

Bond investment is a reliable way to manage financial assets. By understanding your goals and the market, you can achieve stable returns and long-term financial success.

💡 3-Point Summary

  • Bonds are certificates issued by governments, corporations, or public institutions to raise funds, offering fixed interest and principal repayment at maturity.
  • Investors earn through interest income and capital gains. Direct and indirect methods are available for bond investment.
  • Bond prices and interest rates move inversely. Understanding this dynamic is crucial for successful investing.
