Investment Basics, Understanding Portfolios ‚Äã

Investment Feed | 2024.12.11

The concept of portfolios is one of the first things you encounter when studying investment. There‚Äôs no shortage of articles and videos introducing the portfolios of wealthy investors and their so-called golden ratios.

But why are portfolios so crucial? The answer lies in balancing risks with returns in investments. This article breaks down the foundational theory of risk diversification, types of investment assets, and approaches to building a portfolio.

What is a Portfolio? ‚Äã

The term portfolio refers to a collection or briefcase, originating from the Latin words 'foglio' (paper) and 'portare' (to carry). In investment, it signifies a collection of various types of investment assets instead of papers. Diversifying investments across multiple asset types in a portfolio helps reduce investment risks.

Portfolio Theory

  • In 1952, American economist Harry Markowitz published a paper titled "Portfolio Selection," emphasizing the importance of focusing on risks (rather than just returns) in investments.
  • He introduced diversification through portfolio investment as a strategy to reduce risks while increasing returns. His advice: spread investments across multiple asset types‚Äîa concept that aligns with the saying, "Don‚Äôt put all your eggs in one basket."
  • Risks in investment can be categorized into two types:
    • Specific Risk: Risks associated with individual assets or companies, such as revenue fluctuations.
    • Market Risk: Risks affecting the entire market, such as financial crises or inflation.
  • Markowitz argued that portfolio strategies could eliminate at least specific risks, hence calling them "diversifiable risks."

What Types of Assets Can You Invest In? ‚Äã

Investment assets in a portfolio can be broadly categorized into real estate and financial assets.
Real estate refers to immovable properties like houses, buildings, and land.
Financial assets can be further divided based on risk levels into safe assets and risky assets. Let‚Äôs delve deeper into financial assets.

Safe Assets ‚Äã

Safe assets are further classified into liquid assets and cashable assets. Liquid assets can be withdrawn without principal loss, while cashable assets may incur some loss of principal or interest but are still considered safe.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Liquid Assets ‚Äã

Money Market Fund (MMF)

  • A financial product where the asset management company invests deposited money in safe, short-term products and returns the profits to the investor. Typical investments include corporate paper (CP) and certificates of deposit (CD) with maturities of less than one year.

Comprehensive Asset Management Account (CMA)

  • A securities account similar to an MMF. Deposited funds are invested by the securities company, generating returns. It allows utility payments and credit card use, functioning like a regular bank account.

Money Market Deposit Account (MMDA)

  • A bank product similar to a CMA, used like a checking account for daily transactions. Interest rates vary depending on the deposited amount, but rates are generally low for balances below 50 million KRW.

üí° The above products allow flexible deposits and withdrawals and generate interest even for short-term deposits.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Cashable Assets ‚Äã

Savings Deposits

  • Savings accounts can be divided into regular deposits and time deposits. Regular deposits are typical checking accounts linked to debit cards. Time deposits require fixed amounts to be locked in for specific periods, with principal and interest paid at maturity. Withdrawals before maturity are not allowed.

Bonds

  • Bonds are certificates issued by governments, financial institutions, or companies as proof of borrowing money from investors. Unless the issuer defaults, the principal and interest are guaranteed. Bonds can also be traded before maturity for profit.

Insurance

  • In investments, insurance typically refers to savings-type insurance. After paying premiums, you receive the principal along with interest at maturity. However, the long-term nature (5‚Äì10 years) makes it less suitable for those needing liquidity, like young professionals.

Risky Assets ‚Äã

Risky assets are divided into income-generating assets and high-yield assets. Income-generating assets offer potentially higher returns than bank interest rates but come with some risks, while high-yield assets promise significant gains but carry substantial risks of loss.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Income-Generating Assets ‚Äã

Mutual Funds

  • A financial product where an asset management company pools investors‚Äô money to invest in various assets, returning the profits. It allows small investors to diversify and benefit from professional fund managers' expertise.

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)

  • Similar to mutual funds but traded on stock exchanges like individual stocks. Unlike mutual funds requiring subscriptions or redemptions, ETFs offer the flexibility of direct buying and selling.

2Ô∏è‚É£ High-Yield Assets ‚Äã

Stocks

  • Units of ownership in a corporation. Owning shares makes you a part-owner of the company. If the company performs well, share prices rise, allowing you to sell at a profit. However, stocks are volatile and can fluctuate based on the company‚Äôs circumstances.

Derivatives

  • Products whose value depends on the performance of underlying assets such as stocks, bonds, or currencies. Prices can be volatile, but derivatives allow profits even in declining markets through predefined future transactions.

üí° Derivatives and Structured Products

  • Derivatives include futures, options, and swaps:
    • Futures involve contracts to trade at a set price on a future date, regardless of the market price.
    • Options grant the right but not the obligation to buy or sell.
    • Swaps exchange different cash flows, such as interest rate swaps or currency swaps.
  • Structured products, like Equity-Linked Securities (ELS) or Equity-Linked Funds (ELF), are derived from derivatives.

Commodities

  • Raw materials like oil, metals, agricultural products, and natural gas. Investment can be direct (physical assets) or indirect (commodity ETFs and mutual funds).

Cryptocurrencies

  • Cryptocurrencies and NFTs represent digital assets based on blockchain technology. Unlike traditional financial systems, these are decentralized and validated by participants. However, their high volatility raises debates about their viability as investment assets.

How Should You Invest? ‚Äã

How should you structure your portfolio? Diversification strategies include the 100-age rule, the 7:3 rule, and the 6:4 rule. Here‚Äôs a quick overview:

1Ô∏è‚É£ '100-Age' Rule ‚Äã

Formula: (100 - Age) = Investment Percentage (%)

Allocate your funds so that your age corresponds to the percentage in savings, while the remainder is invested.

  • Younger investors can afford higher-risk investments since they have more time to recover losses. Older investors benefit from prioritizing stability.

2Ô∏è‚É£ 7:3 Rule ‚Äã

Invest 70% in safe assets and 30% in risky assets. Even in the event of risky asset losses, the 70% safe assets ensure recovery.

3Ô∏è‚É£ 6:4 Rule ‚Äã

The traditional strategy involves investing 60% in stocks and 40% in bonds. Stocks and bonds often counterbalance each other‚Äîwhen one declines, the other typically rises. However, this strategy faced challenges during COVID-19 as both markets fell simultaneously, leading to debates about its reliability.


In summary, this guide introduced portfolio theory, asset types, and investment methods. Remember, these are not definitive solutions but general strategies. Customize your approach to fit your financial situation and investment preferences.

üí° 3-Point Summary

  • A portfolio diversifies investments across different asset types to reduce risks.
  • Investment assets are broadly categorized into real estate and financial assets, with financial assets further split into safe and risky categories.
  • Portfolio construction strategies include the 100-age rule, 7:3 rule, and 6:4 rule.
