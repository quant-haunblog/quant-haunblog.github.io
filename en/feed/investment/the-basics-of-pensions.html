Understanding Pensions for a Secure Future ​

Investment Feed | 2024.12.16

Concerns about the depletion of the National Pension Fund are growing. While forecasts have traditionally suggested the fund would run dry by 2056, some experts warn it could happen even sooner. This timeline coincides with the retirement years of today’s 20s and 30s. If the fund is exhausted, the future of pension benefits may be uncertain.

Will the National Pension truly become unavailable? Pensions, including the National Pension, are essential for retirement planning. Let’s explore how to build a secure future through pensions.

1️⃣ The Basics of Pensions: A Three-Tier System ​

A pension is defined as a system or fund that allows individuals to save money over a set period, which they can then draw upon during retirement. The concept involves accumulating savings during one’s working years and receiving regular income-like payments after retirement. A stable retirement typically relies on three types of pensions: public pensions (Tier 1), workplace pensions (Tier 2), and private pensions (Tier 3).

🇰🇷 Public Pension ​

  • Designed to ensure basic living standards for all citizens.
  • Operated by the government, making it highly secure.
  • Mandatory for individuals with income.

🏢 Workplace Pension ​

  • Aimed at securing retirement for workers.
  • Available to individuals with employment income and can be managed by the employer or the employee.

🙆‍♂️ Private Pension ​

  • An optional plan for individuals to enhance their quality of life during retirement.
  • Provided by financial institutions like banks, securities firms, and insurance companies.

2️⃣ Public Pension ​

Public pensions, operated as a form of social security, are divided into the National Pension and Special Occupational Pensions. The latter covers specific professions such as civil servants, military personnel, and private school teachers. Here, we’ll focus on the National Pension, which most citizens are required to join.

👴 What is the National Pension? ​

All citizens aged 18 to 59 with an income must enroll in the National Pension scheme. Members contribute 9% of their monthly income, split equally between the employee (4.5%) and the employer (4.5%).

💡 Can you enroll without income?

Yes, the voluntary enrollment system allows individuals like students, military personnel, and homemakers aged 18 to 26 to join. Homemakers must be married to a pension contributor or beneficiary.

A minimum contribution period of 10 years is required to receive an old-age pension upon reaching the qualifying age. The age varies depending on the individual’s year of birth. The amount received is determined by the member’s income and contribution period.

A key feature of the National Pension is its inflation adjustment mechanism, which increases payouts based on rising prices. Pension income is recalibrated to the current monetary value of the individual’s contributions. Additionally, yearly adjustments are made based on the previous year’s inflation rate.

💸 Example: Inflation Adjustment

If you received 1 million KRW per month last year and inflation was 3.0%, your pension will increase by 30,000 KRW to 1.03 million KRW this year.

Deferring pension benefits increases the payout amount. For each month of delay, benefits rise by 0.6%, adding up to a 7.2% annual increase.

？ Will the National Pension Disappear? ​

Projections suggest the National Pension Fund may be depleted around 2056. This is due to an aging population and declining birth rates, leading to fewer contributors and more beneficiaries. Understandably, younger generations worry about future payouts.

However, there’s no need for excessive concern. As long as the country remains stable, pensions will be paid. If the fund runs dry, the government can adopt a pay-as-you-go system, collecting contributions from working generations to cover the deficit.

3️⃣ Workplace Pension ​

🏦 What is a Workplace Pension? ​

Workplace pensions are designed to secure retirement income by managing severance payments. Typically, severance pay is given as a lump sum upon resignation, but in difficult times, companies may struggle to provide this. Workplace pensions ensure stable management by entrusting funds to financial institutions. These pensions are divided into Defined Benefit (DB) and Defined Contribution (DC) plans, with an additional option for individual workers called the Individual Retirement Pension (IRP).

🏛️ Defined Benefit (DB) Plan ​

Formula: Average Monthly Salary (Last 3 Months) × Years of Service

This plan guarantees a predetermined amount of severance pay. Employers invest funds in financial institutions, and the returns are used to provide regular pension payments. The longer the tenure, the greater the payout.

👷‍♂️ Defined Contribution (DC) Plan ​

Formula: Annual Salary ÷ 12 + Investment Gains/Losses

Employers deposit a fixed amount into an employee’s account annually, and employees choose how to invest the funds. The severance amount varies based on investment performance.

💡 DB vs. DC: Which is Better?

  • DB plans offer stability, as the employer manages the funds.
  • DC plans are advantageous during strong market conditions but involve higher risks.

💁‍♂️ Individual Retirement Pension (IRP) ​

The IRP is a flexible pension option available to employees, freelancers, and self-employed individuals. Members can begin receiving benefits from age 55. The IRP allows workers to consolidate severance payments into a single account, even when changing jobs. Since 2022, employers are required to deposit severance into an IRP if no other plan exists.

IRPs also offer tax benefits, including deferred severance taxes and a 30% reduction in tax on withdrawals. Contributions up to 9 million KRW are tax-deductible annually. However, only 70% of contributions can be invested in high-risk assets like stocks to ensure stability.

4️⃣ Private Pension ​

💸 What is a Private Pension? ​

Private pensions are financial products offered by institutions, categorized as tax-qualified and non-tax-qualified plans. Tax-qualified products, like retirement savings plans, provide tax deductions during the contribution period but are subject to taxation upon withdrawal. Non-tax-qualified products, like retirement insurance, do not offer tax benefits but are tax-free upon withdrawal.

📈 Retirement Savings Plans ​

These plans allow withdrawals after five years of enrollment, starting at age 55. Individuals can contribute up to 18 million KRW annually across multiple accounts, with tax deductions available for up to 6 million KRW (or 9 million KRW including IRPs). Early withdrawal, however, requires repayment of any tax benefits received.

Retirement savings plans are available as insurance or funds. Choose insurance for low-risk investments or funds for aggressive growth.

🛡️ Retirement Insurance ​

This is a form of life insurance that provides fixed payouts during retirement or upon death. Unlike retirement savings plans, there is no contribution limit, and withdrawals can begin at age 45. Retirement insurance is further divided into general retirement insurance and immediate annuities.

  • General retirement insurance: Tax-exempt if maintained for 10+ years and paid for 5+ years.
  • Variable retirement insurance: Offers high returns but carries risk of principal loss.

💡3 Key Takeaways

  • A stable retirement relies on public pensions, workplace pensions, and private pensions.
  • Workplace pensions are divided into Defined Benefit (DB), Defined Contribution (DC), and Individual Retirement Pensions (IRP).
  • Private pensions include tax-qualified products like retirement savings plans and non-tax-qualified products like retirement insurance.
