Acting President Han Duck-soo exercised his veto power in a cabinet meeting on four agriculture-related bills, including the amendment to the Grain Management Act. The proposed amendment mandates the government to purchase surplus rice and compensate the price difference when rice prices fall below a certain level.

The government opposes the amendment, citing financial burdens and the issue of overproduction of rice, while farmer organizations argue that it is essential for securing farm incomes and ensuring food security. The bill has been returned to the National Assembly, where it requires the approval of at least two-thirds of the total members to pass upon reconsideration. If the reconsideration fails or the parliamentary session ends, the bill will be discarded.

The Grain Management Act is a law that allows the government to intervene actively in the supply and price stabilization of key grains like rice. First enacted in 1950, this law aims to ensure the income stability of farmers and safeguard national food security. It has been revised over time to adapt to changes in the agricultural environment.

Beyond merely adjusting market prices, this law plays a critical role in protecting farmers' livelihoods and maintaining the nation's food self-sufficiency system.

The Grain Management Act is based on Article 123 of the Constitution, which mandates the state to protect and foster agriculture and farmers. The law provides the government with a legal foundation to intervene in the production and price fluctuations of key agricultural products like rice.

  • Article 16: The government may purchase rice as needed to stabilize supply and demand.
  • Article 26: When rice prices fall below a certain threshold, the government can intervene in the market to stabilize prices.

The Grain Management Act is designed to prevent price crashes caused by rice overproduction and to promote the economic stability of farmers. Government purchase policies help stabilize prices in the agricultural market and maintain food self-sufficiency. However, critics highlight concerns about fiscal burdens and distortions in the market economy.

The proposed amendment to the Grain Management Act could promote the economic stability of farmers but might impose significant financial burdens on the government. When supply exceeds demand, the government must continually purchase surplus rice, leading to increased fiscal expenditures. This could reduce the government's ability to invest in other sectors. Additionally, excessive government intervention might distort market mechanisms and weaken agricultural competitiveness.

Conversely, the amendment provides stable income for farmers and ensures food security. Socially, it positively impacts the livelihoods of agricultural workers and the sustainability of rural communities, though it might spark policy conflicts with urban residents.

Negative Impacts

  • Policy Conflicts: Urban residents may be less supportive of rice price stabilization policies, potentially escalating rural-urban tensions.
