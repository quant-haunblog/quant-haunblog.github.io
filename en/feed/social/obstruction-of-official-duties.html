📰 Obstruction of Official Duties ​

Social News | 2025.01.09

📌 Criticism Directed at the Corruption Investigation Office over the Failed Attempt to Arrest President Yoon Suk-yeol ​

The Corruption Investigation Office for High-ranking Officials (CIO) faced resistance from the Presidential Security Service in its attempt to arrest President Yoon Suk-yeol, leading to its failure. The CIO has been criticized for inadequate preparation and execution issues. Specifically, the lack of strategic planning during the execution of the arrest warrant, legal misinterpretations, and information leaks have been pointed out as factors undermining the principles of lawful arrest. Questions regarding the competence and structural limitations of the CIO have been raised, further intensifying debates over institutional reform and even its potential dissolution.

1️⃣ Definition ​

Obstruction of official duties refers to acts of violence, threats, or other forms of interference that hinder public officials from lawfully performing their duties under the law. This constitutes a serious offense that disrupts the enforcement of public authority, thereby undermining social order and the rule of law, and is strictly regulated and punished.

⚙️ Requirements for Establishing Obstruction of Official Duties ​

1. The Public Official Must Be Performing Their Duties

For obstruction of official duties to be established, the public official must be performing their duties lawfully and within their authorized powers.

  • Example: A police officer arresting a criminal or conducting traffic enforcement.
  • If the duties are not lawful (e.g., abuse of authority), it will not be recognized as obstruction of official duties.

2. Acts of Interference Such as Violence or Threats

Interference includes physical or psychological means such as violence or threats.

  • Violence: Includes physical contact (e.g., punching or pushing a public official).
  • Threats: Verbal intimidation that instills fear or disrupts duties.

3. Connection Between the Act of Interference and the Official’s Duty

  • The act of interference must directly hinder the public official’s performance of their duties. Mere criticism or protest is not considered obstruction of official duties.

2️⃣ Principles and Legal Framework ​

The offense of obstruction of official duties is rigorously defined under criminal law to protect the rule of law and respect for public authority.

📕 Article 136 of the Criminal Act (Obstruction of Official Duties) ​

  • Any person who interferes with the lawful performance of a public official’s duties through violence or threats shall be punished by imprisonment for up to five years or by a fine of up to ten million won.

📕 Article 7 of the Criminal Procedure Act (Legitimacy of Public Authority Enforcement) ​

  • Public duties are protected by law, and any interference with their execution is subject to strict sanctions.

📕 Articles 12 and 37 of the Constitution ​

  • Citizens’ fundamental rights are guaranteed as long as they do not infringe on public order, and the judiciary and executive branches may exercise public authority to ensure this.

Obstruction of official duties is an essential mechanism to safeguard public interest and national order. Violating this principle can have widespread adverse effects on society and undermine the foundations of the rule of law by obstructing or refusing the execution of legitimate warrants.

3️⃣ Impacts ​

Obstruction of official duties has significant legal, social, and political implications.

💡 Legal Impact ​

  • Hindering the lawful execution of a public official’s duties constitutes a criminal offense, which can delay investigations and legal processes. If committed by those in power, it erodes public trust in the legal system.

💡 Social Impact ​

  • Obstruction of official duties weakens the societal consensus that laws must be observed, potentially encouraging other individuals or groups to engage in unlawful acts. This can lead to legal conflicts and public unrest.

4️⃣ Comparison with Related Terms ​

🔎 Neglect of Duty ​

  • Refers to a public official’s failure to perform their duties without justifiable reasons. While obstruction of official duties involves external interference, neglect of duty pertains to internal negligence.

🔎 Disobedience of Orders ​

  • Describes a public official’s refusal to comply with a superior’s orders. Obstruction of official duties typically focuses on external parties interfering with law enforcement.

🔎 Treason ​

  • Involves acts aimed at overthrowing the authority of the state, a far more extensive and severe offense with broader objectives and consequences than obstruction of official duties.
